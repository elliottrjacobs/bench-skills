# Design Principles & Craft

Distilled design philosophy and craft principles. Sources: Bob Baxley, Katie Dill, Karri Saarinen, Dylan Field, Jessica Hische.

---

## Bob Baxley — 35 Years of Design Wisdom

Source: Lenny's Podcast. Apple, Pinterest, ThoughtSpot.

### What Design Actually Is
- "Design is clear thinking made visible" (Edward Tufte)
- Design is trying to imagine the future you want to live in and then take the steps to make it real
- A company being "design-led" does NOT mean "designer-led" — it's a mindset, not a role
- Design-led culture is baked in at founding — you can't graft it on later

### Tenets over Principles
Design principles (simple, clear, beautiful) are nice but useless as decision-making tools — nobody argues the opposite. **Design tenets** are decision-making tools that create real trade-offs.

**Example tenets (ThoughtSpot):**
1. "Documentation is a failure state" — If users need a manual, we failed
2. "Every interaction should start simple — users opt into complexity" — Counter to Tableau's power-user-first approach
3. "The entire product should look and feel like it came from a single mind" — Combats enterprise fragmentation

**How to write good tenets:**
- Must imply a real trade-off (someone could reasonably argue the opposite)
- Max 3-4 tenets (everyone must memorize them, no consulting a handbook)
- Should resolve recurring design debates

### The Beatles Principle
You get The Beatles with 4 people. You don't get it with 8 or 24. Small teams create "scenius" (Brian Eno's term) — collective genius. The original Mac had 20 people. The iPhone patent has 24 names. When you need to figure out something new, keep the team small.

### Values vs. Behaviors
When changing companies/cultures: hold onto the *values* (attention to detail, product excellence) but recalibrate the *behaviors* to fit the new culture. Apple's directness worked at Apple but not at Pinterest.

### Design Under Engineering
Apple had design reporting to engineering under Steve Jobs. Counterintuitive but effective:
- Design as "phase zero" of engineering — tighter coupling, fewer surprises
- Better cost and timeline estimation
- Engineers feel ownership from the start
- Not one-size-fits-all, but worth considering as an option alongside design-as-independent and design-under-product

### Getting Buy-In (or Better: Ownership)
"Buy-in" is agreeing with someone else's idea. What you want is **ownership** — people feeling it's part of their soul. Achieve this by:
- Bring engineering and product in at the beginning, not after design is "done"
- Never present a fully-baked design for "approval" — bring people along
- Find the moment when key people "fall in love" with the concept
- "Everybody in product, design, engineering is fundamentally a maker. They all want to make something they're proud of."

### Wait Before Drawing
Resist the urge to sketch or prototype too early. Spend time in the conceptual/philosophical space first:
- What are we trying to achieve?
- What are the tenets?
- What's the right conceptual model?
- Only after alignment on direction should you start drawing

## Katie Dill — Beauty Drives Business

Source: Lenny's Podcast. Stripe Head of Design.

### Core Premise
Beauty and craft aren't luxury — they're competitive advantage. Stripe's design quality directly contributes to trust, adoption, and retention.

### Quality Systems
- Design quality must be systematized, not left to individual taste
- Design systems (component libraries, style guides) are infrastructure, not decoration
- The system should make the right thing easy and the wrong thing hard
- Regular quality reviews where the whole team evaluates shipped work against standards

### Craft as Signal
- The quality of your product signals the quality of your company
- In B2B especially, design quality = trust signal
- "If they can't get the UI right, can I trust them with my data?"

## Karri Saarinen — Taste, Craft, Focus

Source: Lenny's Podcast. Linear co-founder.

### Opinionated Design
- Linear is deliberately opinionated — it doesn't try to be everything to everyone
- "We build the tool we want to use" — the best products come from scratching your own itch
- Say no to most feature requests. Every feature you add dilutes focus.

### Craft Signals Care
- Animations, transitions, keyboard shortcuts — these "small" things signal that the team cares
- Speed is a feature — a fast interface shows respect for the user's time
- "Good taste is recognizing quality" — it's a learnable skill through exposure and practice

### Focus
- Do fewer things, better
- The roadmap should fit on one page
- Kill features that don't serve the core job

## Scott Belsky — First Mile Experience

Source: Lenny's Podcast. Adobe CSO, Behance founder.

### The First Mile
The first 30 seconds of a product experience determine everything. Most products obsess over features for power users while ignoring the first-time experience.

**First mile principles:**
- New users should get value within 60 seconds
- Reduce decisions on the first screen — guide, don't ask
- Show the outcome before asking for setup
- The first mile is your most important design surface

### The Messy Middle
Between the excitement of starting and the satisfaction of finishing is "the messy middle" — where most projects die. Design can help by:
- Breaking large tasks into visible progress steps
- Celebrating small wins
- Making the next action obvious

## Design Review — How to Evaluate Work

### Evaluation Criteria
When reviewing design work, assess across these dimensions:

1. **Clarity** — Can users understand what to do without instruction?
2. **Consistency** — Does it feel like part of the same product?
3. **Craft** — Are the details polished? Spacing, alignment, typography?
4. **Hierarchy** — Is the most important information most prominent?
5. **Efficiency** — Can users accomplish their goal with minimum friction?
6. **Delight** — Is there anything that surprises or pleases?
7. **Tenets alignment** — Does it follow the product's design tenets?

### Review Format
- Start with what's working (pattern recognition of quality)
- Identify the biggest structural issue (not a laundry list of nits)
- Offer specific direction, not just critique ("Try moving X above Y to establish hierarchy" not "This feels off")
- Ask questions before prescribing solutions ("What were you optimizing for here?")
