# Placek Naming Methodology

Reference notes from David Placek's approach at Lexicon Branding. Lexicon has created some of the most recognized brand names in technology: Sonos, Pentium, Blackberry, Swiffer, Dasani, PowerBook.

## Core Principles

### 1. Sound Symbolism Matters
Certain sounds evoke specific feelings:
- **Hard consonants** (K, T, P) — power, precision, technology (Kodak, TikTok, Pentium)
- **Soft consonants** (S, L, M) — smooth, elegant, flowing (Sonos, Luma, Silk)
- **Vowel openness** — open vowels (A, O) feel expansive; closed vowels (I, E) feel precise
- **Consonant clusters** — create memorability through distinctive sound patterns

### 2. The "Network Effect" of Names
A great name should:
- Be easy to spell after hearing it
- Be easy to pronounce after reading it
- Work in multiple languages (avoid unintended meanings)
- Sound natural in "Have you tried [name]?" — the word-of-mouth test

### 3. Avoid the Descriptive Trap
Descriptive names (e.g., "FastShip", "SmartBuild") are:
- Easy to understand but hard to own
- Difficult to trademark
- Limiting as the product evolves
- Forgettable because they blend with competitors

The strongest names create meaning through use, not through description.

### 4. The Alphabet Advantage
Placek's research shows:
- Names starting with uncommon letters (X, Z, Q) are more distinctive
- Short names (1-2 syllables) are more memorable
- Names with repeated sounds create rhythm (Coca-Cola, TikTok, Lululemon)

## Evaluation Framework

### The "Morning After" Test
Would someone remember this name the morning after hearing it once at a dinner party?

### The "Cocktail Party" Test
Can you naturally say "I work at [name]" or "Have you tried [name]?" without it feeling awkward?

### The "Logo" Test
Can you visualize this as a logo? Does it have visual distinctiveness?

### The "Search" Test
Is it Google-able? Does searching for it return the intended result, or generic content?

### The "Global" Test
Does it have negative meanings in major languages? (Check Spanish, French, German, Mandarin, Japanese at minimum for global products.)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Committee naming** — Too many stakeholders dilute the name into blandness
2. **Overthinking meaning** — The name doesn't need to explain the product
3. **Ignoring sound** — How a name sounds matters more than what it means
4. **Domain obsession** — Don't reject a great name because .com is taken; alternatives work
5. **Playing it safe** — Boring names are the riskiest choice because they're forgettable
