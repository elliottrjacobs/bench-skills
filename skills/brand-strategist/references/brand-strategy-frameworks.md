# Brand Strategy Frameworks

Detailed methodology reference for the studio brand strategist. These frameworks come from leading practitioners, many drawn from Lenny's Podcast interviews.

---

## April Dunford — Positioning

Source: *Obviously Awesome*, Lenny's Podcast episodes on positioning and sales pitch.

### The 5 Components of Positioning

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers do if you didn't exist? This is the true competitive set. Not just direct competitors — often includes spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring someone, doing nothing.
2. **Unique Attributes** — What do you have that alternatives don't? Features, capabilities, approach, business model.
3. **Value** — What do those unique attributes actually deliver for the customer? (Not features — the outcome the features enable.)
4. **Target Audience** — Who cares most about that value? The customer segment that makes your differentiation most relevant.
5. **Market Category** — The context you position in so customers can understand what you are quickly.

### Positioning Drives Everything

- "40-60% of B2B purchases end in no decision" — buyers are overwhelmed and afraid of making the wrong choice
- Your job is to help buyers make a confident decision, not just pitch features
- Customers want perspectives on the market and help weighing options
- If your insight doesn't resonate with a prospect, they're probably not your target customer — disqualify, don't force it

### Sales Pitch Structure (from positioning)

**Setup (the market, not you):**
1. **Insight** — Your point of view on the market. The problem inside the problem. ("Customer service is different for digital businesses — it's a growth driver, not a cost center.")
2. **Alternatives** — Pros and cons of each approach customers could take. Paint the full picture honestly.
3. **Perfect World** — "Can we agree that an ideal solution would tick these boxes?" Get alignment on criteria before showing your product.

**Follow-through (your differentiated value):**
4. **Introduction** — Who you are, one sentence
5. **Value → How** — For each differentiator: here's the value, here's how we deliver it
6. **Proof** — Case studies, third-party validation, data
7. **Objections** — Handle silent concerns (cost, complexity, migration)
8. **The Ask** — What happens next

### Key Principles
- The setup should be a conversation, not a monologue — ask "do you agree?" along the way
- Talk about value, not features
- Start with what the customer would do if you didn't exist
- If you can't tell someone what you do in one sentence, you have a positioning problem

## Arielle Jackson — Brand Framework

Source: Lenny's Podcast, First Round Review articles, Maven course on startup brand strategy.

### Three Pillars: Purpose, Positioning, Personality

#### Purpose
- **Format:** "We exist to [change you want to see in the world]"
- **Timeframe:** 10 years — this doesn't change often
- **Test:** Can you say it introducing yourself at a conference? Does it make people want to hear more?
- **Test 2:** Could it be the header of your About page?

**Purpose Discovery Exercise:**
1. List all cultural tensions happening in the world relevant to your business
2. List all ways you'd describe your brand's best self
3. Pick the best articulation from each side
4. With that context, complete: "The world would be a better place if..."
5. Then complete: "We exist to..."

**Examples:**
- Google: "To organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful"
- Stripe: "To increase the GDP of the internet"
- Nike: "To bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world" (+ "if you have a body, you are an athlete")

#### Positioning
- **Format:** "For [target audience] who [statement of need/opportunity], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [current alternative], our product [key differentiator]."

**Process:**
1. Who is this for? (Concentric circles: TAM → segments → target audience → model persona)
2. What's their problem?
3. How do they address it today?
4. What do you make? How does it work?
5. What would you want a user to tell another user?

**The Bar Test:** Pretend to be someone in your target audience at a bar talking to a friend. Say: "I just started using [product]. It's this really great [category] that [benefit]." If the friend says "tell me more" and you explain your differentiator — and it all sounds like something a human would actually say — you've nailed it.

**Key insight:** Your benefit should be what you want someone to tell someone else. "Turn your iPad into a point of sale" — that's what you'd want a Square merchant to say at a bar.

#### Personality

**Step 1: Jennifer Aker's 5 Brand Personality Dimensions**
- **Sincerity** — Down to earth, honest, wholesome, cheerful
- **Excitement** — Daring, spirited, imaginative, up-to-date
- **Competence** — Reliable, intelligent, successful
- **Sophistication** — Upper class, charming, glamorous
- **Ruggedness** — Outdoorsy, tough, Western

Strong brands spike in **2 of 5**. Most tech companies land on sincerity + competence (Google, Amazon). Apple spikes in sophistication + competence.

**Step 2: 5 Personality Attributes as "We are X, but not Y"**
- Y is taking X too far — it creates tension and specificity
- "Playful, but not silly" (Google)
- "Daring, but not reckless"
- "Expert, but not elitist"
- "Warm, but not saccharine"
- "Bold, but not aggressive"

These should create interesting tension. If all 5 say the same thing in different words, you haven't done the work.

### Naming (from Jackson)

**Naming Brief:**
1. What are you naming? (Company, product, both?)
2. What do you want the name to communicate?
3. What do you want to avoid?
4. Names of competitive/related products
5. Additional considerations (pronunciation in other languages, etc.)

**7 Universal Criteria:**
1. Trademark availability
2. Domain availability
3. Distinctiveness — Is it memorable and different?
4. Timelessness — Avoid naming trends (the -ly era, vowel-dropping era)
5. Messaging alignment — Reflective of key messaging or emotion
6. Sound and pronunciation — Fun to say, easy to spell
7. Appearance — Visual design potential, letter shapes

**Name Spectrum:**
- Descriptive (Internet Explorer) → Suggestive (Chrome) → Evocative (Seesaw, Maven) → Empty Vessel/Fanciful (Yahoo, Kodak)
- Suggestive/evocative names do marketing work for you
- Empty vessel names need more marketing investment but can become powerful over time
- "A bad name won't kill a good company" — Disney, Volvo, Nike were all unremarkable names that became iconic through consistent brand building

## Bob Moesta — Jobs to Be Done

Source: Lenny's Podcast, *Demand Side Sales*, *Job Moves*.

### Core Premise
People don't buy products — they hire them to make progress in their life. Understanding the "job" reveals the true competitive set and the real reasons people switch.

### The 4 Forces of Progress

```
F1: Push of the situation (context driving change)
F2: Pull of the new solution (magnetism of what's possible)
    ─────────────────────────────
F3: Anxiety of the new (fear, uncertainty about switching)
F4: Habit of the present (comfort, familiarity, sunk cost)
```

**F1 + F2 must be greater than F3 + F4 for change to happen.**

- Adding more features (F2) isn't always the answer — it can increase anxiety (F3)
- Reducing friction (F3 + F4) can be more effective than adding pull
- Example: Selling condos to downsizers — including moving + 2 years storage (reducing F3/F4) increased sales 30%, even at a higher price

### Key Principles

- **Context makes the irrational rational.** When behavior seems strange, you don't have the full story yet.
- **Struggling moments create demand, not products.** Supply doesn't create demand — a struggling moment does.
- **The real competitive set comes from the job.** Snickers competes with protein shakes and coffee (meal replacement job), not with Milky Way (emotional comfort job).
- **It's not pain and gain — it's context and outcome.** A vector of progress, not a binary.
- **Study people who recently switched**, not people who say they want to. What people say they want and what they actually do are different. (93% said they wanted Energy Star homes; 0% bought them — they all bought the finished basement.)

### 6 Phases of Buying
1. First Thought
2. Passive Looking (problem aware, solution unaware)
3. Active Looking (problem and solution aware)
4. Deciding (making trade-offs)
5. First Use
6. Ongoing Use (building the new habit)

Meet customers where they are in this timeline, not where you want them to be.

## Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative

Source: Lenny's Podcast, "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" (Medium).

### Core Shift: Movement, Not Problem-Solution

Traditional pitch: "You have a problem → we have a solution → here's why we're better"
Strategic narrative: "The world is changing → here's the new game → winners play the new game → we help you win"

This defines a **movement**, not just a product. It becomes the north star for product, sales, marketing, fundraising, and recruiting.

### The Framework

**1. Name the shift — Old Game → New Game**
- Software → Cloud (Salesforce)
- Transactions → Subscriptions (Zuora)
- Opinions → Reality (Gong)
- Must be concise — name both the old and new in 1-2 words each
- It's okay to overstate slightly; it's a narrative frame, not a research paper

**2. Name the stakes**
- Show winners already playing the new game
- Show losers stuck in the old game
- Make it feel like life and death (in business terms)
- "Companies that don't adapt are dying" + "Look at all these winners who already moved"

**3. Show the promised land**
- Paint a picture of what winning in the new game looks like
- This is aspirational but specific
- Not about your product — about the world your customers want to live in

**4. Introduce obstacles**
- What's preventing people from reaching the promised land on their own?
- This is where the need for your product becomes clear — but you haven't pitched yet

**5. Present your solution as the way to the promised land**
- Now (and only now) you talk about what you offer
- Frame it as "we help you play the new game" not "we solve your problem"

### Key Principles
- The CEO should be telling this story everywhere — it's not just a sales deck
- Your product roadmap should map to the journey from old game to new game
- If your narrative doesn't resonate, you may be talking to the wrong audience (same as Dunford's disqualification principle)
- Category creation is limiting — narrative creation is more powerful
- This structure works because it mirrors how movies work: shift in the world → stakes → hero's journey
