# Copywriting & Content Marketing Frameworks

Distilled from established practitioners. Use the right framework for the right content type — don't force every piece through the same structure.

---

## Classic Copy Structures

### PAS — Problem, Agitation, Solution
Best for: Ads, email subject lines, social hooks, short-form copy.

1. **Problem** — Name the pain the reader already feels
2. **Agitation** — Twist the knife. Make the cost of inaction vivid.
3. **Solution** — Present your offer as the natural resolution

*Use when the audience already knows they have a problem. Gets to the point fast.*

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
Best for: Landing pages, long-form ads, sales emails, blog CTAs.

1. **Attention** — Interrupt with a headline that earns the next sentence
2. **Interest** — Build relevance. "This is about you."
3. **Desire** — Show the transformation. Social proof, specifics, outcomes.
4. **Action** — Clear CTA. One ask, not three.

*Use for longer persuasion arcs where you need to build a case.*

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge
Best for: Case studies, testimonials, transformation stories, email openers.

1. **Before** — The world before your solution (the struggle)
2. **After** — The world after (the outcome)
3. **Bridge** — How you get from Before to After (the product/service)

*Use when you have strong before/after contrast. Great for case studies.*

## Eugene Schwartz — 5 Levels of Awareness

From *Breakthrough Advertising*. Match your copy to where the reader is:

| Level | Reader's State | Your Job | Headline Approach |
|-------|---------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Most Aware** | Knows your product, just needs the deal | Make the offer | Direct: price, bonus, urgency |
| **Product Aware** | Knows you exist, not convinced | Prove your claims | Benefit + proof: "Here's why..." |
| **Solution Aware** | Knows solutions exist, not yours | Position your solution | Lead with the mechanism: "The [method] that..." |
| **Problem Aware** | Feels the pain, doesn't know solutions | Agitate then solve | Lead with the problem: "Tired of...?" |
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know they have a problem | Educate through story | Lead with story or identity: "If you're a [type]..." |

**Key principle:** You cannot make someone aware. You can only meet them where they are and lead them one level forward.

## David Ogilvy — Headline & Copy Principles

### Headlines
- On average, 5x more people read the headline than the body. If the headline doesn't sell, you've wasted 80% of the spend.
- Include the brand name. Include the benefit. Be specific.
- Headlines with news ("introducing", "now", "finally") outperform clever wordplay.
- Numbers and specifics beat generalities: "Reduces costs by 37%" beats "Saves money."

### Body Copy
- Long copy sells more than short copy when the reader is interested. Don't be afraid of length — be afraid of boring.
- First sentence must compel reading the second sentence. Every sentence earns the next.
- Write the way you talk. Then edit for clarity, not formality.
- Specifics persuade. "731 companies" beats "hundreds of companies."

## Donald Miller — StoryBrand Framework

Best for: Website copy, landing pages, brand messaging, about pages.

Every brand story has 7 elements:

1. **A Character** — The customer (not your brand). What do they want?
2. **Has a Problem** — External (surface), Internal (how it makes them feel), Philosophical (why it's wrong)
3. **Meets a Guide** — Your brand. Show empathy ("We understand") and authority ("We've helped 500+ companies")
4. **Who Gives Them a Plan** — 3-step process. Makes it feel simple and safe.
5. **And Calls Them to Action** — Direct CTA ("Buy now") + Transitional CTA ("Download the guide")
6. **That Helps Them Avoid Failure** — What's at stake if they don't act?
7. **And Ends in Success** — The transformation. What does winning look like?

**Key principle:** The customer is the hero. Your brand is the guide. If you position yourself as the hero, you're competing with your customer for the same role.

## Joanna Wiebe — Conversion Copywriting

From Copyhackers. Best for: Landing pages, CTAs, A/B test copy, signup flows.

### Voice of Customer (VoC) Mining
- Pull exact language from: customer reviews, support tickets, survey responses, Reddit/forum threads, competitor reviews
- The best copy isn't written — it's assembled from what customers already say
- Look for: emotional language, "struggling moment" descriptions, before/after framing

### CTA Principles
- Complete the sentence: "I want to ___"
- Bad: "Submit" / "Sign up" / "Learn more"
- Good: "Start my free trial" / "Get the playbook" / "See my results"
- The CTA should express the value, not the action

### Landing Page Hierarchy
1. Hero: Headline (benefit) + subhead (mechanism) + CTA
2. Social proof bar (logos, numbers, quote)
3. Problem agitation (3 pain points)
4. Solution introduction (what it does)
5. How it works (3 steps)
6. Proof (case study, data, testimonials)
7. Final CTA with objection handling

## Robert Cialdini — 6 Principles of Persuasion

From *Influence*. Apply to any content type:

| Principle | In Copy |
|-----------|---------|
| **Reciprocity** | Give value first. Free resources, insights, tools. |
| **Commitment/Consistency** | Start with small asks. "Do you agree that...?" |
| **Social Proof** | Testimonials, user counts, logos, case studies. "Join 10,000+ teams" |
| **Authority** | Expert endorsements, credentials, data from credible sources. |
| **Liking** | Relatability, shared values, brand personality. Write like a human. |
| **Scarcity** | Limited availability, deadlines, exclusive access. Use honestly. |

## Ann Handley — Content Marketing Rules

From *Everybody Writes*. Best for: Blog posts, newsletters, brand content.

- **Utility x Inspiration x Empathy** — Great content sits at the intersection of all three.
- **"So what?" test** — After every sentence, ask "So what does this mean for the reader?" If you can't answer, cut it.
- **Write for one person.** Not an audience. One specific reader. Use "you."
- **Start with the end.** What should the reader do, feel, or know after reading?
- **The Ugly First Draft is mandatory.** Write freely, then edit ruthlessly. Good writing is rewriting.
- **Show, don't tell.** Replace adjectives with specifics. Not "industry-leading" — explain why.

## Joe Pulizzi — Content Marketing Strategy

From *Content Inc.* Best for: Blog strategy, newsletter strategy, content calendars.

### Content Tilt
- Don't just cover a topic. Find the angle no one else owns.
- "Content marketing for SaaS" is a topic. "Content marketing for bootstrapped B2B SaaS under $10M ARR" is a tilt.
- The tilt is where you have expertise AND where there's a content gap.

### One Channel, One Format
- Start by dominating one platform with one format before expanding.
- Blog OR newsletter OR podcast OR YouTube. Not all four at once.
- Build an audience in one place, then syndicate.

## Platform-Specific Conventions

### LinkedIn
- Hook in first 1-2 lines (before "see more" fold)
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences max)
- White space between paragraphs
- Personal perspective > corporate voice
- End with a question or clear CTA
- 1,200-1,500 characters for feed posts; up to 3,000 for articles
- Hashtags: 3-5 max, at the end

### Twitter/X
- 280 characters. Front-load the value.
- Threads: Hook tweet → supporting points → CTA
- Hot take or contrarian angle performs best
- No hashtags in the tweet body (looks spammy)
- Quote tweet > reply for reach

### Instagram
- Visual-first. Copy supports the image/video.
- Caption hook in first line
- Stories: conversational, polls, questions
- Reels: hook in first 3 seconds
- Hashtags: 5-10 relevant ones

### Email
- Subject line: 6-10 words, specific, curiosity or benefit-driven
- Preview text: extends the subject line, not repeats it
- One CTA per email (primary). One goal.
- P.S. line gets read more than body copy — use it
- Mobile-first: short paragraphs, scannable

### Google Ads
- Headline 1: Include keyword + benefit (30 chars)
- Headline 2: Differentiator or proof point (30 chars)
- Headline 3: CTA or brand (30 chars)
- Description: Expand on benefit, include CTA (90 chars)
- Use all available extensions

### Meta Ads (Facebook/Instagram)
- Primary text: Hook in first 125 characters (before truncation)
- Headline: Benefit-focused (40 chars)
- Description: Supporting detail (30 chars)
- Visual: The image/video does the heavy lifting — copy supports
