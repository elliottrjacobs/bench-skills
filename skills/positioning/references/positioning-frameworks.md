# Positioning Frameworks

Distilled methodology for product and company positioning. Sources: April Dunford, Arielle Jackson, Andy Raskin, Geoffrey Moore, Christopher Lochhead.

---

## April Dunford — Positioning Methodology

Source: *Obviously Awesome*, Lenny's Podcast (2 episodes).

### The 5 Components of Positioning

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers do if you didn't exist? Not just direct competitors — includes spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring someone, doing nothing.
2. **Unique Attributes** — What do you have that alternatives don't? Features, capabilities, approach, business model.
3. **Value** — What do those unique attributes actually deliver? Not features — the outcome they enable.
4. **Target Audience** — Who cares most about that value? The segment that makes your differentiation most relevant.
5. **Market Category** — The context you set so customers quickly understand what you are.

### Key Principles
- "40-60% of B2B purchases end in no decision" — buyers are overwhelmed and afraid of making the wrong choice
- Your job is to help buyers make a confident decision, not just pitch features
- If your insight doesn't resonate with a prospect, they're probably not your target — disqualify, don't force it
- Start with what the customer would do if you didn't exist — that's your true competitive set

### Positioning Drives the Sales Pitch

**Setup (the market, not you):**
1. **Insight** — Your point of view on the market. The problem inside the problem.
2. **Alternatives** — Pros and cons of each approach. Paint the full picture honestly.
3. **Perfect World** — "Can we agree an ideal solution would have these qualities?" Get alignment before showing your product.

**Follow-through (your differentiated value):**
4. **Introduction** — Who you are, one sentence.
5. **Value → How** — For each differentiator: the value, how you deliver it.
6. **Proof** — Case studies, data, third-party validation.
7. **Objections** — Handle silent concerns (cost, complexity, migration).
8. **The Ask** — What happens next.

## Arielle Jackson — Brand Framework

Source: First Round Review, Lenny's Podcast, Maven course.

### Three Pillars: Purpose, Positioning, Personality

#### Purpose
- **Format:** "We exist to [change you want to see in the world]"
- **Timeframe:** 10 years — doesn't change often
- **Test:** Can you say it at a conference and make people want to hear more?

**Discovery exercise:**
1. List cultural tensions relevant to your business
2. List ways you'd describe your brand's best self
3. Pick best from each side
4. Complete: "The world would be a better place if..."
5. Complete: "We exist to..."

#### Positioning Statement
**Format:** "For [target audience] who [need/opportunity], [product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [alternative], our product [differentiator]."

**The Bar Test:** Pretend to be someone in your target audience at a bar. Say: "I just started using [product]. It's this great [category] that [benefit]." If a friend says "tell me more" and the differentiator flows naturally — you've nailed it.

#### Personality (Jennifer Aaker's 5 Dimensions)
- **Sincerity** — Down to earth, honest, wholesome
- **Excitement** — Daring, spirited, imaginative
- **Competence** — Reliable, intelligent, successful
- **Sophistication** — Upper class, charming, glamorous
- **Ruggedness** — Outdoorsy, tough, Western

Strong brands spike in **2 of 5**. Write 5 personality attributes as "We are X, but not Y" — the Y creates tension and specificity.

### Name Spectrum
Descriptive (Internet Explorer) → Suggestive (Chrome) → Evocative (Seesaw) → Fanciful (Kodak)

## Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative

Source: "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" (Medium), Lenny's Podcast.

### The Framework: Movement, Not Problem-Solution

**1. Name the shift — Old Game → New Game**
- Software → Cloud (Salesforce)
- Transactions → Subscriptions (Zuora)
- Opinions → Reality (Gong)
- Concise: name both old and new in 1-2 words each

**2. Name the stakes**
- Show winners already playing the new game
- Show losers stuck in the old game
- Make it feel like life and death (in business terms)

**3. Show the promised land**
- What winning in the new game looks like
- Aspirational but specific
- About the world your customers want — not about your product

**4. Introduce obstacles**
- What prevents people from reaching the promised land on their own?
- This is where need for your product becomes clear — but you haven't pitched yet

**5. Present your solution as the way**
- Now you talk about what you offer
- Framed as "we help you play the new game" not "we solve your problem"

### Key Principles
- The CEO should tell this story everywhere — not just in sales
- Product roadmap should map to the journey from old game to new game
- Category creation is limiting — narrative creation is more powerful
- This mirrors how movies work: world shift → stakes → hero's journey

## Geoffrey Moore — Crossing the Chasm

Source: *Crossing the Chasm*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Beachhead Strategy
Don't try to win the whole market. Pick one narrow segment (the beachhead) and dominate it completely before expanding.

**Beachhead selection criteria:**
- Segment has a compelling reason to buy (urgent problem, budget, authority)
- You can deliver a whole product for this segment (not just a partial solution)
- Word of mouth within the segment is strong (they talk to each other)
- Winning this segment creates a reference base for expanding to adjacent segments

### The Chasm
Between early adopters and the early majority is a "chasm" — early adopters buy vision; the mainstream buys proven solutions with references. You cross the chasm by:
1. Choosing a beachhead segment
2. Building the whole product for that segment (not just the core product — the entire solution including services, support, integrations)
3. Creating compelling references within that segment
4. Using those references to expand to adjacent segments

### Positioning Statement (Moore's Template)
"For [target segment] who [statement of need], [product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [primary alternative], [product] [primary differentiation]."

## Christopher Lochhead — Category Design

Source: *Play Bigger*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Core Premise
Category kings capture 76% of the economics in their category. The most valuable companies don't win existing categories — they create new ones.

### Category Design vs. Positioning
- **Positioning** = how you fit within an existing category
- **Category design** = creating a new category that you define and lead

### The Lightning Strike
A deliberate moment where you introduce the new category to the world:
- Name the category
- Define the problem the category solves
- Position yourself as the category leader
- Educate the market on why the old way doesn't work

### When to Create vs. Compete
**Create a new category when:**
- Existing categories misframe the problem
- You'd be forced into unfavorable comparisons
- The market shift you're riding is fundamental enough to warrant a new frame

**Compete in an existing category when:**
- The category is well-understood and large
- You have a clear differentiator within the category
- Creating a new category would confuse more than it helps

### Key Principles
- "If you don't design your category, someone else will design it for you"
- Category names should be 2-3 words max
- The company that frames the problem wins
- Category design is a CEO-level activity, not marketing
