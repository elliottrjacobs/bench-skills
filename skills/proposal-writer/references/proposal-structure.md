# Proposal Structure

Distilled methodology for writing persuasive client proposals. Sources: Andy Raskin, Matt Dixon, Wes Kao, Nancy Duarte.

---

## Andy Raskin — Narrative Structure for Proposals

Source: "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" (Medium), Lenny's Podcast.

### Applied to Proposals
The same strategic narrative that works in a pitch deck works even better in a proposal, because a proposal has more space to develop the argument.

**Proposal narrative flow:**
1. **The Shift** — The world is changing. Name the old game and new game relevant to this client.
2. **The Stakes** — Companies adapting are winning. Those stuck in the old game are at risk.
3. **The Promised Land** — Paint a specific picture of what success looks like for THIS client.
4. **The Obstacles** — What's preventing them from getting there on their own.
5. **Our Approach** — How we help them get to the promised land (scope, methodology, team).
6. **Proof** — Evidence we've done this before (case studies, results).
7. **Investment** — Pricing, framed as investment against the value delivered.
8. **Next Steps** — Clear, specific action to move forward.

**Key principle:** The proposal should feel like a story about the client's future, not a catalog of your services.

## Matt Dixon — Reducing Buyer Indecision

Source: *The JOLT Effect*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Applied to Proposals
40-60% of qualified deals are lost to no decision. Your proposal must actively reduce indecision, not just present information.

**Anti-indecision tactics in proposals:**
- **Offer your recommendation** — Don't present 5 options. Present THE recommended approach with clear reasoning. "Based on what we've discussed, here's what I'd recommend."
- **Limit scope creep** — Define clear boundaries. "Here's what's included. Here's what's not."
- **Take risk off the table** — Include guarantees, milestones, off-ramps. "If we don't hit [milestone] by [date], we pause and reassess."
- **Address the 3 fears:**
  1. Wrong choice → "Here's why this approach vs. alternatives"
  2. Surprise costs → "Fixed scope and fixed price — no surprises"
  3. No ROI → "Here are the specific metrics we'll track to prove value"

## Wes Kao — Sales Before Logistics

Source: Lenny's Podcast. Maven co-founder.

### The MOO Framework
**MOO = Most Obvious Objection.** Before you send anything, identify the #1 reason the reader might say no, and address it head-on.

### Sales Before Logistics
Structure proposals so the **value and vision come first**, logistics and details come second.

**Wrong order:**
1. About Us
2. Scope of Work
3. Timeline
4. Pricing
5. Why You Should Work With Us

**Right order:**
1. Your Situation (show you understand them)
2. The Opportunity (what's possible)
3. Our Approach (how we get there)
4. Expected Results (what they'll get)
5. Investment (pricing, framed as investment)
6. About Us (proof we can deliver)
7. Next Steps

### Writing Principles
- Lead with the client's problem, not your capabilities
- Use specific numbers and examples, not vague claims
- Write at a 6th-grade reading level — clarity is persuasion
- Every paragraph should pass the "so what?" test
- Bold the key points — executives skim

## Nancy Duarte — Contrast Structure

Source: *Resonate*, Lenny's Podcast.

### "What Is / What Could Be" in Proposals

Throughout the proposal, alternate between the client's current reality and the future possibility:

```
Current State: "Today, your team spends 15 hours/week on [task]."
Future State: "With [solution], that drops to 2 hours — freeing 13 hours for [higher-value work]."

Current State: "Your conversion rate is 2.3%, below the industry benchmark of 4.1%."
Future State: "Our approach has helped similar companies reach 5-6% within 90 days."
```

This contrast creates desire. The gap between "what is" and "what could be" is the energy that drives a yes.

## Proposal Template Structure

### Section 1: Executive Summary (1 page)
- The shift/opportunity relevant to this client
- What we recommend
- Expected outcomes
- Investment overview
- Timeline

### Section 2: Understanding (1-2 pages)
- Client's current situation (demonstrate you've listened)
- The challenge or opportunity they face
- What they've tried or considered
- Why now — the urgency or timing

### Section 3: Our Approach (2-3 pages)
- Methodology overview (not a feature list — a process)
- Phase breakdown with deliverables per phase
- Key milestones and decision points
- What we need from the client (their investment of time/access)

### Section 4: Expected Outcomes (1 page)
- Specific, measurable outcomes
- Timeline to results
- How we'll measure success together
- Case study or proof point from similar engagement

### Section 5: Investment (1 page)
- Pricing (framed against the value delivered — the ROI ratio)
- What's included
- What's not included (manages scope expectations)
- Payment terms
- Options (if appropriate — Good/Better/Best)

### Section 6: Why Us (1 page)
- Relevant experience (not your full bio — what's relevant to THIS project)
- Team members on this project
- Brief case studies (3 sentences each, focused on results)

### Section 7: Next Steps (half page)
- Specific next action ("Sign and return by [date]")
- Who to contact
- Timeline to kick off

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **The Capability Dump** — Listing everything you can do instead of what's relevant to this client
2. **The Template Feel** — Generic language that could apply to any client (no specifics about them)
3. **Burying the Price** — Making them hunt for the cost signals you're not confident in it
4. **No Urgency** — Not explaining why now matters
5. **The Committee Proposal** — Written by multiple people with inconsistent voice and no narrative thread
6. **Defensive Pricing** — Apologizing for the price or over-justifying it signals low confidence
