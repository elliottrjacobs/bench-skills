Distilled pricing and monetization methodology. Sources: Madhavan Ramanujam, Patrick Campbell, Naomi Ionita.

## Madhavan Ramanujam — Pricing from 400+ Companies

Source: *Monetizing Innovation*, *Scaling Innovation*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Core Thesis: Dual-Engine Growth
To build an enduring business, master both **market share** (acquisition) and **wallet share** (monetization). This is not a choice — you need both. But you don't put equal effort on both at all times; you put equal **attention** on both.

### The 3 Archetypes (and Their Traps)

**Disruptor** (market-share focused):
- Trap 1: Land but don't expand — gave away too much, nothing to upsell to
- Trap 2: Win market share but don't hold it — churn because not investing in existing customers

**Moneymaker** (wallet-share focused):
- Trap 3: Nickel and dime — too many charges, hidden fees, customer feels milked
- Trap 4: Price premium paradox — price so high it kills acquisition

**Community Builder** (retention focused):
- Trap 5: Focus on foundation, miss the frontier — so focused on loyal base you forget to grow
- Trap 6: Train customers to expect more for less — always giving more to keep loyal users happy

**Goal:** Be all three simultaneously — a "profitable growth architect."

### Beautifully Simple Pricing (Startup Phase)
- Your pricing must be simple enough that a customer can explain it back to you
- Pricing should tell a **value story** — contextualize the price against the value delivered
- Example: Superhuman at $30/month = "a dollar a day for 4 hours of productivity back per week"
- Example: Subway $5 footlong = simple, value-clear
- **Test:** Can your customer sell your pricing on your behalf? If they can't articulate it simply, it's too complex.

### The Pricing Model 2x2 (AI Era)

| | Low Attribution | High Attribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Low Autonomy** | Per-seat / flat fee | Usage-based |
| **High Autonomy** | Subscription tiers | **Outcome-based** ★ |

- **Top-right quadrant (outcome-based) is the holy grail** — you charge based on the value delivered
- Only ~5% of companies are in true outcome-based pricing today
- "If you want to win in AI, figure out a way to get to that quadrant"
- AI makes outcome-based pricing possible because AI can do the work AND measure the result

### AI Pricing Principles
- AI pricing is different because cost structure is variable (inference costs per query)
- "If you're bringing a lot of value to the table and you start training customers to expect $20/month, you're in trouble"
- Don't anchor on a low price point early — it's extremely hard to raise later
- "20% of what you build drives 80% of willingness to pay — and that 20% is often the easiest thing to build"
- The winners in AI will master monetization from day one

### Mastering Negotiations (Scale-Up Phase)

**1. Gives and Gets**
- Never give a concession without getting something back
- Top get: **Value audit** — customer commits to measuring your impact every 6 months, creating their own business case for renewal
- Giving without getting signals you can be beaten down more

**2. Value Selling**
- **Create needs** (don't just discover them) — "So this process takes 3 weeks? What if it was instant?"
- **Create affirmation loops** — Pause after showing value: "How does this play out in your company?"
- **Co-create the ROI model** from day one — agree on inputs during POC, so they can't argue the output
  - 3 ROI buckets: incremental gains (revenue, retention), cost savings (headcount, licenses), opportunity cost (what they do with saved time)

**3. Negotiation Tactics**
- **Show up with options** — Good/Better/Best shifts conversation from price to value
- **The courage hack** — Offer: "$100K + 10% of incremental value" OR "$500K fixed." The $500K is the premium for certainty. In 80% of cases, they negotiate the fixed fee (often landing at $400K) rather than taking the outcome-based option.
- **Anchor high** — Starting high means ending higher
- **Taper concessions** — 15% → 5% → 2% signals negotiation is ending. 5% → 10% → 15% signals you can be beaten up.

### 9 Strategies (Overview)
**Startup phase:** Beautifully simple pricing, land and expand design, packaging strategy, pricing model selection
**Scale-up phase:** Master negotiations, stop churn before it happens, effective price increases, segment-specific pricing, outcome-based models

## Patrick Campbell — SaaS Pricing

Source: Lenny's Podcast. ProfitWell founder (acquired by Paddle).

### Value Metric
The unit of pricing that scales with the value the customer receives:
- **Good value metrics:** Users, contacts, API calls, revenue managed, events tracked
- **Bad value metrics:** Features, storage (usually — unless you're a storage company)
- Test: Does the customer naturally use more of this as they get more value?

### Willingness to Pay Research
Ask 4 questions (Van Westendorp):
1. At what price would this be a bargain? (too cheap)
2. At what price would this be getting expensive?
3. At what price would it be too expensive?
4. At what price would it be so cheap you'd question the quality?

Plot the curves — the intersection points define your acceptable price range.

### Pricing Page Principles
- 3-4 tiers max (more causes decision paralysis)
- Name tiers after the customer, not the features ("Startup", "Growth", "Enterprise")
- Highlight the recommended tier
- Annual discount should be 15-20% (enough to incentivize, not so much it devalues)
- Enterprise = "Contact us" (custom pricing signals premium)

### Retention > Acquisition
- Improving retention by 1% has 2-5x more impact than improving acquisition by 1%
- Involuntary churn (failed payments) is 20-40% of total churn — fix it first
- Expansion revenue from existing customers should be a core growth engine

## Naomi Ionita — Monetization Mistakes

Source: Lenny's Podcast. Menlo Ventures.

### Usage-Based vs. Seat-Based
- **Seat-based** works when: value scales linearly with users, easy to understand, low buyer friction
- **Usage-based** works when: value varies widely across users, heavy users should pay more, AI/API products
- **Hybrid** (seat + usage) is increasingly common — base seats + usage overage

### Common Monetization Mistakes
1. **Pricing too low** — Underpricing signals low value and is nearly impossible to recover from
2. **Pricing too late** — "We'll figure out pricing later" means you're building without validation
3. **Wrong value metric** — If your metric doesn't scale with value, you'll under-monetize power users and over-charge light users
4. **No expansion path** — If there's no natural reason for customers to pay more over time, your LTV flatlines
5. **Copying competitors** — Their pricing reflects their cost structure and positioning, not yours
