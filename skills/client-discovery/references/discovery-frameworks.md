# Discovery Frameworks

Distilled methodology for customer and market discovery. Sources: Teresa Torres, Bob Moesta, Rahul Vohra, Gia Laudi.

---

## Teresa Torres — Continuous Discovery

Source: *Continuous Discovery Habits*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Core Premise
Discovery and delivery should happen in parallel, continuously — not in phases. The goal is at least one customer interview per week, every week, forever.

### Opportunity Solution Trees

A visual framework connecting business outcomes to customer opportunities to solutions to assumption tests:

```
Business Outcome (metric you're trying to move)
├── Opportunity 1 (unmet need / pain point / desire)
│   ├── Solution A
│   │   ├── Assumption Test 1
│   │   └── Assumption Test 2
│   └── Solution B
├── Opportunity 2
│   └── Solution C
└── Opportunity 3
```

**Key distinctions:**
- **Opportunities are NOT solutions.** "I need a faster checkout" is an opportunity. "Add Apple Pay" is a solution. Never skip to solutions.
- **Opportunities come from customer stories**, not from asking "what do you want?"
- **Multiple solutions per opportunity** — compare and contrast before committing
- **Assumption tests are cheap experiments**, not full builds

### Experience Mapping
Map the customer's current experience step by step:
1. What triggers them to start?
2. What steps do they take?
3. Where do they get stuck or frustrated?
4. What workarounds do they use?
5. What does success look like for them?

This reveals opportunities at each friction point.

### Interview Methodology
- **Collect stories, don't ask opinions.** "Tell me about the last time you..." not "What do you think about..."
- **Anchor in specific past behavior.** People can't predict future behavior but can recall past actions.
- **One interview per week minimum** — automate recruiting through product opt-ins ("Would you be willing to chat with us?")
- **Interview in pairs** — reduces bias, captures more
- **Synthesize immediately** — map insights to opportunity solution tree right after

### Key Principles
- "The biggest mistake teams make is jumping to solutions before understanding the opportunity space"
- Discover continuously alongside delivery — not in separate phases
- Compare at least 3 solutions before committing to build one
- Test assumptions before building — identify the riskiest assumption and test it first

## Bob Moesta — Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Source: *Demand Side Sales*, *Job Moves*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Core Premise
People don't buy products — they hire them to make progress in their life. Understanding the "job" reveals the true competitive set and the real reasons people switch.

### The 4 Forces of Progress

```
DRIVING CHANGE:
  F1: Push of the situation (context driving change — pain, frustration, new circumstance)
  F2: Pull of the new solution (magnetism of what's possible — hope, aspiration)

RESISTING CHANGE:
  F3: Anxiety of the new (fear, uncertainty — "will this work?", "what if I fail?")
  F4: Habit of the present (comfort, familiarity, sunk cost — "I know how this works")
```

**F1 + F2 must be greater than F3 + F4 for someone to switch.**

- Adding more features (F2) isn't always the answer — it can increase anxiety (F3)
- Reducing friction (F3 + F4) can be more effective than adding pull
- Study people who recently switched, not people who say they want to

### 6 Phases of Buying
1. **First Thought** — Something triggers awareness
2. **Passive Looking** — Problem-aware, solution-unaware
3. **Active Looking** — Problem and solution aware
4. **Deciding** — Making trade-offs
5. **First Use** — Onboarding, setup
6. **Ongoing Use** — Building the new habit

Meet customers where they are in this timeline, not where you want them to be.

### Discovery Interview Technique
- Interview people who recently switched (bought/churned/changed)
- Ask them to walk through the timeline: "When did you first think about this?"
- Map all 4 forces for each interview
- **Context makes the irrational rational** — if behavior seems strange, you don't have the full story yet

### Key Insight
The real competitive set comes from the job, not the category. Snickers competes with protein shakes and coffee (meal replacement job), not Milky Way (emotional comfort job).

## Rahul Vohra — Product-Market Fit Engine

Source: Lenny's Podcast, "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product-Market Fit."

### The PMF Survey
Ask users: **"How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"**
- Very disappointed
- Somewhat disappointed
- Not disappointed

**Benchmark:** If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have product-market fit. Below 40%, you don't.

### The PMF Engine (Process)

**Step 1: Segment.** Don't look at all users. Find the segment where PMF score is highest. These are your ideal users. Double down on them.

**Step 2: Analyze what they love.** Ask the "very disappointed" group: "What is the main benefit you get?" Their words become your positioning and marketing language.

**Step 3: Understand what holds others back.** Ask the "somewhat disappointed" group: "What would make [product] better for you?" This is your roadmap — but filtered.

**Step 4: Build only for your ideal users.** Deliberately ignore feedback from the "not disappointed" group. They're not your target. Building for them dilutes what makes you great.

**Step 5: Track the score over time.** Run the survey regularly. Watch the PMF score climb as you improve for your ideal segment.

### Key Principles
- "You have to deliberately NOT act on the feedback of many early users"
- Segment relentlessly — PMF is per-segment, not overall
- Use customers' own words for positioning (what the "very disappointed" group says they love)
- Word of mouth is the true growth engine — "remarkable quality" means people can't help but tell others

### Contrarian Pricing Insight
Superhuman charged $30/month from day one — competing with free Gmail. The framing: "A dollar a day for 4 hours of productivity back per week." Value story > price comparison.

## Gia Laudi — Customer-Led Growth

Source: Lenny's Podcast, *Forget the Funnel*.

### Core Premise
Growth comes from deeply understanding your best customers' journey and using their language in your marketing, positioning, and product.

### The Customer-Led Journey Map
Map the experience of your best customers (not all customers) through:

1. **Struggle** — What was happening in their life/work that made them look for a solution?
2. **Evaluation** — How did they find you? What alternatives did they consider? What criteria mattered?
3. **Purchase/Signup** — What pushed them over the edge? What almost stopped them?
4. **Onboarding** — What was their first experience? When did they first feel value?
5. **Retention** — What keeps them coming back? What would make them leave?
6. **Advocacy** — What makes them tell others? What words do they use?

### Using Customer Language
- Mine interviews and support tickets for the exact phrases customers use
- Use those phrases in headlines, CTAs, onboarding copy
- "If your marketing copy sounds like marketing, you've done it wrong"
- The best positioning comes from how your happiest customers describe you to their friends

### Key Insight
Your best customers already know what your value prop should be — you just need to listen and mirror their language back to the market.
