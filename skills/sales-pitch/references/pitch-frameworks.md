# Pitch Frameworks

Distilled methodology for building sales pitches, cold emails, and demo scripts. Sources: April Dunford, Matt Dixon, Andy Raskin, Nancy Duarte, Pete Kazanjy.

---

## April Dunford — The Sales Pitch Structure

Source: *Obviously Awesome*, Lenny's Podcast.

### 8-Step Pitch (from Positioning)

**Setup (the market, not you):**
1. **Insight** — Your point of view on the market. The problem inside the problem. ("Customer service is different for digital businesses — it's a growth driver, not a cost center.")
2. **Alternatives** — Pros and cons of each approach customers could take. Be honest — this builds credibility.
3. **Perfect World** — "Can we agree that an ideal solution would have these qualities?" Get alignment on criteria before showing your product.

**Follow-through (your differentiated value):**
4. **Introduction** — Who you are, one sentence.
5. **Value → How** — For each differentiator: here's the value, here's how we deliver it. Lead with value, not features.
6. **Proof** — Case studies, third-party validation, data. "Companies like [similar to prospect] saw [specific result]."
7. **Objections** — Handle the silent concerns proactively: cost, complexity, migration, risk.
8. **The Ask** — What happens next. Be specific: "Can we schedule a 30-minute technical deep dive with your team next Tuesday?"

### Key Principles
- The setup should be a **conversation**, not a monologue — ask "do you agree?" along the way
- Talk about **value, not features** — "This saves you 10 hours/week" not "This has automated scheduling"
- Start with what the customer would do if you didn't exist
- Create **affirmation loops** — pause and get agreement before moving to the next point

## Matt Dixon — JOLT Method

Source: *The JOLT Effect*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Core Insight
40-60% of qualified deals are lost to **no decision** — not to competitors. The #1 enemy isn't the competition, it's the buyer's indecision.

### Why Buyers Don't Decide
It's not FOMO (fear of missing out) — it's **FOMU (fear of messing up)**:
- **Omission bias** — Doing nothing feels safer than doing something wrong
- **3 fears that paralyze:**
  1. Fear of choosing the wrong option
  2. Fear of learning something new after deciding (buyer's remorse from new information)
  3. Fear of not seeing the expected ROI

### The JOLT Framework

**J — Judge the level of indecision**
- Use "pings and echoes" — ask probing questions to gauge how stuck the buyer is
- Look for signals: asking the same questions repeatedly, requesting more references, stalling on next steps
- High indecision requires different tactics than low indecision

**O — Offer your recommendation**
- Don't give buyers more options — this increases indecision
- Use the **delegation effect**: "Based on what you've told me, here's what I'd recommend."
- Buyers want guidance, not a menu. They hired you as the expert.
- "If I were in your shoes, I'd start with [specific option] because [reason tied to their situation]."

**L — Limit the exploration**
- Set a boundary on the evaluation process: "We've covered the major scenarios. I want to make sure we're not over-analyzing."
- **Radical transparency** — proactively share limitations: "Here's what we're not great at. Here's where [competitor] might be better."
- This builds trust and stops the endless research loop

**T — Take risk off the table**
- Address the 3 fears directly:
  - Wrong choice → "We can start with a pilot / POC with defined success criteria"
  - New information → "We lock in this price for 12 months regardless of what changes"
  - No ROI → "We include a 90-day success review with your team"
- Make it safe to say yes. Reduce the cost of being wrong.

### Key Insight
"The best sellers don't overcome objections — they prevent indecision." Adding more information, more demos, more references often makes indecision worse, not better.

## Andy Raskin — Strategic Narrative as Pitch

Source: "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" (Medium), Lenny's Podcast.

### The 5-Step Narrative Pitch

1. **Name the shift** — "[Industry] is changing. The old game was [X]. The new game is [Y]."
2. **Name the stakes** — "Companies playing the new game are winning. Those stuck in the old game are struggling."
3. **Show the promised land** — "Imagine a world where [specific outcome the buyer wants]."
4. **Introduce obstacles** — "But getting there is hard because [specific obstacles]."
5. **Present your solution** — "That's why we built [product]. We help you [play the new game]."

### Why This Works
- It creates a movement, not just a product pitch
- The buyer feels part of something bigger than a transaction
- It mirrors movie structure: world shift → stakes → hero's journey
- The "old game → new game" frame makes the status quo feel dangerous

## Nancy Duarte — Presentation Storytelling

Source: *Resonate*, Lenny's Podcast.

### The "What Is / What Could Be" Structure
Alternate between the current state and the future state:

```
What Is (current reality — the pain)
  → What Could Be (the vision — the possibility)
What Is (another aspect of the pain)
  → What Could Be (another aspect of the vision)
What Is (the cost of inaction)
  → What Could Be (the transformation)
Call to Action
```

This creates rhythm and tension. The audience keeps oscillating between discomfort (current state) and hope (future state), building desire for the solution.

### Key Principles
- The audience is the hero, not you
- Use specific, concrete examples — not abstract claims
- Every slide should pass the "glance test" — understand the point in 3 seconds
- End with a clear, specific call to action

## Pete Kazanjy / Jen Abel — Founder-Led Sales

Source: *Founding Sales*, Lenny's Podcast.

### Why Founders Must Sell First
- No one knows the product and vision like the founder
- Early sales conversations are discovery — they refine the product
- You can't hire a salesperson until you've closed the first 10-20 deals yourself
- The sales process you develop becomes the playbook for your first hire

### Cold Outreach That Works
- **Subject line:** Specific, relevant, personal (not "Quick question" or "Intro")
- **First line:** Reference something specific about them (not "I love your company")
- **The hook:** One sentence connecting their situation to your value
- **The ask:** Low-commitment next step ("Would a 15-minute call make sense?")
- Keep it under 100 words. Fewer is better.

### Demo Script Structure
1. **Re-confirm the problem** (2 min) — "Last time we spoke, you mentioned [specific pain]. Is that still the case?"
2. **Show the promised land** (3 min) — What their world looks like with your solution (not features — outcomes)
3. **Live demonstration** (15 min) — Walk through their specific use case, not a generic demo
4. **Proof** (5 min) — "Here's how [similar company] achieved [specific result]"
5. **Next steps** (5 min) — Propose specific next action and timeline

### Key Principles
- "Sales is not convincing — it's helping the buyer buy"
- Always tie back to the buyer's specific situation
- Ask questions more than you make statements
- The best demo is one where the buyer says "Can we try that with our data?"
