## Buying Committee Mapping

Most B2B purchases involve 6-10 people. Map each role:

### The 5 Buying Committee Roles

**1. Champion** — The person who finds you and advocates internally
- Typically: Individual contributor or team lead who feels the pain daily
- Motivation: Solve their personal problem, look smart for finding the solution
- Win them: Show immediate value for their workflow, make them the hero internally
- Risk: They may not have budget authority or organizational influence

**2. Economic Buyer** — Controls the budget
- Typically: VP, Director, C-suite depending on deal size
- Motivation: ROI, risk reduction, strategic alignment
- Win them: Business case, ROI model, peer references, risk mitigation
- Risk: May not understand the day-to-day problem

**3. Technical Evaluator** — Assesses whether it works
- Typically: IT, Engineering, Security, Architecture team
- Motivation: Integration ease, security, maintenance burden, technical fit
- Win them: Technical docs, API quality, security certifications, sandbox/POC
- Risk: Can veto on technical grounds even if business case is strong

**4. End User** — Will use the product daily
- Typically: The team that will adopt the tool
- Motivation: Ease of use, better than current workflow, minimal disruption
- Win them: Intuitive UX, migration support, training resources
- Risk: Can resist adoption and cause churn post-sale

**5. Blocker** — Someone who can kill the deal
- Typically: Procurement, Legal, a competing internal solution owner, or a skeptic
- Motivation: Risk avoidance, protecting their domain, process compliance
- Neutralize: Address their concerns proactively, provide compliance docs, show that inaction is riskier

### Buying Committee by Deal Size

| Deal Size | Typical Committee | Decision Process |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| <$10K | Champion + Economic Buyer (same person) | Credit card, 1-2 week cycle |
| $10K-$50K | Champion + Economic Buyer + Technical | Demo → POC → Sign-off, 1-3 months |
| $50K-$250K | Full committee (5-7 people) | Formal eval → POC → Procurement, 3-6 months |
| $250K+ | Full committee + Legal + Executive sponsor | RFP → Multi-round eval → Board approval, 6-12 months |

## Negative Persona Framework

Negative personas prevent wasted sales and marketing effort. Define who NOT to sell to:

### Identification Criteria

1. **High churn segment** — Which customer types consistently churn within 6 months?
2. **Support drain** — Which types generate disproportionate support tickets?
3. **Long sales cycle, low close rate** — Which deals take forever and usually don't close?
4. **Misaligned use case** — Who buys your product for something it's not designed for?
5. **Price sensitivity mismatch** — Who consistently pushes for discounts below your floor?

### Negative Persona Template

```
Name: [Descriptive name, e.g., "The Feature Requester"]
Company profile: [Size, industry, stage that doesn't fit]
Title: [Typical job title]
Why they look like a fit: [What makes them seem like a prospect]
Why they aren't: [The underlying misalignment]
Warning signs in sales: [Red flags during the sales process]
Disqualification criteria: [Specific, measurable criteria to filter them out]
```

## Language Bank Methodology

The language bank captures exact phrases buyers use. This is the most valuable research output because it directly feeds outreach, content, and sales scripts.

### Sources (in priority order)

1. **Customer interviews** — Richest source. Record and mine transcripts.
2. **G2 / Capterra / TrustRadius reviews** — Of your product AND competitors
3. **Reddit / forums** — r/[industry], Hacker News, specialized forums
4. **Support tickets** — How customers describe problems in their own words
5. **LinkedIn posts** — From people with target titles discussing the problem space
6. **Job postings** — What companies hiring for related roles say about challenges
7. **Sales call recordings** — How prospects describe their situation

### Organization Structure

| Category | What to Capture | Example |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| **Problem language** | How they describe the pain in their words | "We're drowning in spreadsheets" |
| **Outcome language** | How they describe the desired state | "I just want one source of truth" |
| **Trigger language** | How they describe what prompted the search | "After our third data incident, we knew..." |
| **Objection language** | How they express concerns | "My team would never adopt another tool" |
| **Decision criteria** | What they say matters when evaluating | "Integration with Salesforce is non-negotiable" |
| **Value language** | How current users describe the benefit | "It saved us 10 hours a week on reporting" |

### Usage

- **Outreach:** Use problem and trigger language in cold email hooks
- **Content:** Use problem language in headlines, outcome language in CTAs
- **Sales:** Use their vocabulary, not yours — mirror their language
- **Positioning:** Build messaging pillars from value language patterns

## Buying Trigger Taxonomy

Events that cause buyers to enter the market:

| Category | Triggers |
|----------|---------|
| **Growth** | Hiring spike, new funding round, revenue milestone, new market entry |
| **Pain** | Compliance failure, data breach, audit finding, customer complaint wave |
| **Change** | New leadership (CTO, VP), reorg, digital transformation initiative |
| **Competitive** | Competitor launched something, losing deals on capability gap |
| **Lifecycle** | Contract renewal coming up, current vendor acquired/sunset, tool consolidation |
| **Seasonal** | Budget planning cycle, fiscal year start, industry event season |

Use triggers to:
- Build signal-based prospecting criteria
- Time outreach to trigger events
- Create content that maps to trigger moments
